Opening a Bank Account and Borrowing Money in Hungary as an Expat

How to open a Hungarian bank account, get a mortgage as a foreigner, and understand the financial regulation landscape.

Banking for Expats in Hungary: Opening Accounts, Mortgages, and Financial Services

Establishing banking relationships is one of the first practical necessities for expats moving to Hungary. Whether you're opening a personal bank account, obtaining a mortgage for property purchase, or managing business finances, understanding the Hungarian banking system is essential. This guide covers account opening procedures, mortgage options, financial services available to expats, and how to find English-speaking banking assistance.

Opening Bank Accounts as an Expat

Opening a personal bank account in Hungary as an expat is straightforward, though requirements vary by bank. Major Hungarian banks include OTP Bank, Erste Bank, K&H Bank, and Rabobank. Many international banks also operate in Hungary.

To open an account, you typically need: valid identification (passport or national ID card), proof of address in Hungary (tenancy agreement, utility bill, or registration of residence), and proof of income (employment contract or business documentation). Some banks may require additional documentation for non-EU nationals.

Many banks have English-speaking staff in larger branches, particularly in Budapest. Some banks offer online account opening with video verification, which can expedite the process. Branches and online banking are standard, allowing you to manage your account remotely.

Banks may charge monthly account maintenance fees (typically 500 to 3,000 HUF or 1.50 to 10 EUR annually), though many offer fee-free accounts to customers meeting specified criteria (such as direct deposits above certain amounts).

Business Banking and Company Accounts

If you have a Hungarian business, opening a business bank account is required for most corporate structures. Business accounts are offered by most banks and require: company registration documents, articles of association, identification of beneficial owners, and proof of business address.

Business accounts typically have higher fees than personal accounts and may include services such as invoicing, payroll processing, and transaction monitoring. Some banks offer specialised services for startups and small businesses.

You are required to deposit company capital into a business bank account during company registration. Your lawyer or accountant can advise on account opening procedures and bank selection for your business.

Mortgages and Property Financing

Many foreign expats finance property purchases through Hungarian mortgages. Hungarian banks have become increasingly willing to lend to foreign nationals, though requirements are stringent.

Typical mortgage requirements include: 20% to 30% down payment, proof of stable income (employment contracts or business documentation), clean credit history, and proof of funds for down payment. Non-Hungarian employment income is increasingly accepted, provided it is documented and stable.

Mortgage interest rates are variable and linked to BUBOR (Budapest Interbank Offered Rate) or Euribor. Current rates typically range from 6% to 9% depending on loan-to-value ratio and market conditions. Loan terms typically range from 15 to 25 years.

The application process involves property appraisal, credit assessment, and final approval. Processing typically takes 2 to 4 weeks. Some banks require that you maintain accounts with them, which can simplify the process if you have existing banking relationships.

Alternative financing options include mortgages from your home country (if lenders permit), private loans from investors, or cash purchases. Consult a financial advisor about the most cost-effective financing option for your situation.

Currency Exchange and International Transfers

Many expats receive income in foreign currencies and need to exchange funds to Hungarian forints. Banks offer currency exchange services, though rates may not be optimal. Specialist currency exchange providers (such as Wise, OFX, and others) often provide better rates for international transfers than banks.

If you transfer funds internationally regularly, compare rates and fees among multiple providers. Currency fluctuations can significantly affect the amount received, so timing and exchange rate management are important.

SEPA transfers (EU-wide bank transfers) are standardised and relatively inexpensive for transfers within Europe. Non-European transfers may be more expensive and slower. Direct deposits from foreign employers typically involve SEPA or SWIFT transfers, which can take several days to settle.

Payment Methods and Daily Banking

Hungary is increasingly cashless, with debit and credit cards widely accepted. Contactless payments are ubiquitous, and mobile payment apps are becoming standard. ATMs are readily available throughout Hungary, though not all accept foreign cards without fees.

Cheques are rarely used in modern Hungary and are not recommended for payments. Bank transfers are the standard method for significant payments. Online banking platforms allow easy transfer management.

POS card payments (debit and credit card payments at retailers) are standard and secure. Most merchants accept international cards, though some smaller establishments may require Hungarian bank cards.

Savings and Investment Options

Banks offer savings accounts with interest, though rates are modest. Deposit insurance protects accounts up to 100,000 EUR per depositor per bank under EU deposit guarantee schemes, so funds are safe even if a bank fails.

Brokerage services for stock and bond investments are available through banks and specialised brokers. Investment advice should be sought from qualified financial advisors. Foreign investment income may trigger additional tax obligations, so consult a tax advisor.

Pension products are available, and contributions to private pension plans may provide tax benefits. However, pension regulations are complex, and you should understand the implications before establishing pension products.

Insurance and Protection Services

Banks often offer insurance products including: life insurance, property insurance, and car insurance. Shopping around for insurance is advisable, as rates vary significantly.

Fraud protection and dispute resolution services are available from all regulated banks. If unauthorized transactions occur, notify your bank immediately. Banks are generally liable for unauthorized transactions if you report them promptly.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How long does it take to open a bank account in Hungary?
A: The process typically takes 1 to 3 business days for in-person applications, or may be completed within hours through online account opening with video verification.

Q: Can I get a mortgage from a Hungarian bank if I am not a Hungarian resident?
A: Yes, though requirements are stricter. You typically must provide proof of stable income, down payment of at least 20%, and may be required to have Hungarian employment or an existing banking relationship.

Q: What are typical mortgage interest rates in Hungary?
A: Variable rates typically range from 6% to 9% depending on the loan-to-value ratio and current market conditions. Fixed rates may be available at slightly higher rates.

Q: Are my bank deposits protected if the bank fails?
A: Yes, up to 100,000 EUR per depositor per bank under EU deposit guarantee schemes. Funds above this amount are not protected.

Q: Can I use my foreign debit card in ATMs throughout Hungary?
A: Yes, most ATMs accept international cards, though you may be charged fees by your home bank or the ATM operator. Having a local Hungarian debit card reduces fees.

Q: What is the best way to transfer funds from abroad to Hungary?
A: For regular transfers, specialist providers like Wise offer better exchange rates than banks. SEPA transfers within Europe are standard. Compare rates among multiple providers for best value.

Understanding Polish Banking Regulations

Poland's banking system is governed by the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (KNF) and operates under both domestic and EU regulations. For expats, navigating banking and finance law in Poland requires understanding a framework that has been substantially harmonised with European standards since EU accession in 2004.

The key legislation includes the Banking Law Act of 1997 (as amended), the Payment Services Act, and various KNF regulations. International firms and individuals benefit from Poland's alignment with EU directives, including MiFID II, PSD2, and the Capital Requirements Directive.

Foreign nationals looking to establish banking relationships in Poland will generally need to provide a valid passport or national ID, proof of address, and potentially documentation of the source of funds. Banks are required to conduct customer due diligence under anti-money laundering regulations.

Corporate Finance and Investment

Poland is one of Central Europe's most active markets for corporate finance, mergers and acquisitions, and private equity investment. The Warsaw Stock Exchange (GPW) is the largest in the region, and Poland attracts significant foreign direct investment across manufacturing, technology, and services sectors.

Expat investors and business owners should be aware of the specific regulatory requirements for foreign investment, including any sector-specific restrictions and reporting obligations. An experienced Polish banking and finance lawyer can assist with structuring transactions to optimise both legal compliance and tax efficiency.

Common transactions handled by Polish banking and finance lawyers include syndicated loans, bond issuances, project finance for infrastructure and real estate, and leveraged buyouts. Foreign lenders and investors should ensure their documentation complies with Polish law requirements, particularly around security interests and enforcement procedures.

Mortgages and Property Financing

For expats purchasing property in Poland, obtaining a mortgage can be straightforward if you meet lender requirements. Polish banks generally offer mortgages to foreign nationals with a valid residency permit and demonstrable income. Interest rates are tied to WIBOR (Warsaw Interbank Offered Rate) for PLN mortgages or EURIBOR for EUR-denominated loans.

A banking lawyer can review mortgage documentation, advise on currency risk for foreign-currency loans, and ensure the security documentation (typically a mortgage registered in the land and mortgage register) properly protects the lender's interests. For commercial property acquisitions, more complex financing structures are common.

It is advisable to engage a legal adviser before signing any loan documentation, particularly if the agreement is presented only in Polish. Expats should pay attention to early repayment clauses, variable rate mechanisms, and any cross-collateralisation provisions.

Compliance and Regulatory Matters

Financial services firms operating in Poland must comply with KNF regulations and EU-level requirements. Brexit has created additional complexity for UK-based firms previously passporting services into Poland, and many have needed to establish EU entities or seek new authorisations.

Individual expats working in financial services should ensure their personal regulatory registrations are in order, including any requirements for professional qualifications recognised in Poland. The KNF maintains registries of authorised intermediaries, investment advisers, and other regulated professionals.

Anti-money laundering compliance has become increasingly important, with Polish institutions required to maintain robust KYC (Know Your Customer) procedures. Expats dealing with significant sums or complex financial structures should seek legal advice to ensure full compliance with reporting obligations.
