Ado az Ingatlan Eladas Utan (Capital Gains Tax on Property)

When selling Hungarian property, any gain above the acquisition cost is subject to 15% personal income tax — though the taxable gain reduces by 10% for each year of ownership beyond the second year, reaching zero after five years.

Hungary levies 15% personal income tax (SZJA) on the profit from selling real property. The gain is calculated as the sale price minus the original acquisition cost plus documented improvement costs. No separate capital gains tax exists — property profits are simply included in annual SZJA.

A crucial relief applies for long-held property: the taxable base is reduced by 10% for each complete year of ownership after the second year. After five full years of ownership, the gain is entirely tax-free. This makes the five-year holding period a significant threshold for sellers.

The acquisition cost is the price recorded in the original deed of sale. If the property was inherited or received as a gift, a special valuation formula applies. Documented renovation costs can be added to the acquisition cost — keep all invoices, as they directly reduce your tax bill.

If a seller reinvests the entire proceeds into another property purchase within 12 months, a partial SZJA deferral (home reinvestment relief) may apply. This relief was modified in 2023, so verify the current rules with a tax adviser.

Non-resident sellers are subject to the same 15% rate on Hungarian-source property gains, but a double-taxation treaty between Hungary and the seller's country of residence may reduce or eliminate the liability. Always consult a cross-border tax specialist before selling.

Key Facts

Tax rate15% SZJA on net gain
Reduction10% per year after year 2; zero liability after 5 years
Deductible costsAcquisition price + documented improvement costs
Reinvestment reliefPartial deferral if proceeds reinvested in 12 months
Non-residentsSame rate; DTA treaties may apply

Common Mistake

Watch out: Many sellers forget to include renovation costs in the acquisition base. Without invoices, you cannot deduct them — save every receipt for work done on the property.

Expert Tip

Pro tip: If you are approaching the five-year mark, consider waiting until the anniversary passes before completing the sale. Selling in month 59 versus month 61 can mean a tax bill of several million forints.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does the five-year rule apply from purchase or from registry?

From the date of the original deed of sale — or, for inherited property, from the date of death of the previous owner.

What if I sell at a loss?

A loss on property cannot be offset against other income in Hungary. It can only reduce gains on other property sold in the same tax year.

Do I need to file a tax return?

Yes. The gain must be declared in your annual SZJA return for the year of sale, even if the entire gain is tax-exempt due to the five-year rule.
