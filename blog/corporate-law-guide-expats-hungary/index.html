Setting Up a Business in Hungary: A Complete Guide for Foreign Entrepreneurs

Starting a business in Hungary as a foreign entrepreneur is increasingly straightforward, particularly for those establishing limited liability companies in Budapest and other major cities. Hungary's legal and regulatory framework welcomes business formation, and the process is relatively efficient by European standards. This guide covers everything you need to know about company formation, tax registration, employment obligations, and finding English-speaking lawyers to support your venture.

Why Hungary Is Attractive for Foreign Business Owners

Hungary offers several advantages for international entrepreneurs. The cost of living and operating costs are lower than Western Europe, making it attractive for startups and scale-ups. As an EU member state, businesses registered in Hungary can operate across the entire EU single market. The educated workforce, particularly in Budapest, includes many English speakers and professionals with international experience. Additionally, Hungary has a developed digital infrastructure and a growing technology sector.

The Hungarian government has introduced various incentives for business investment, including research and development tax credits and employment subsidies in certain sectors. Understanding these opportunities can significantly reduce your startup costs.

Choosing Your Business Structure

The most common business structure for foreign entrepreneurs in Hungary is the limited liability company (Korlátolt Felelősségű Társaság, abbreviated Kft.). This structure offers liability protection, meaning your personal assets are protected from company debts. The minimum capital requirement is relatively low (typically around 3,000 EUR), and the structure is flexible and widely recognised by banks and business partners.

Alternative structures include sole proprietorships (egyéni vállalkozó), which are simpler but offer no liability protection, and joint-stock companies (Zártkörűen működő részvénytársaság), which are more complex and typically used by larger businesses or those planning to raise capital.

For most foreign entrepreneurs starting out, the Kft. is the optimal choice. An English-speaking lawyer can advise you on the best structure for your specific situation, considering your business model, growth plans, and tax implications.

Company Registration Process

Registering a limited liability company in Hungary involves several steps. First, you must prepare articles of association (alapszabály) and register the company at the Court of Registration (Cégbíróság). The process typically takes 7 to 10 working days. Most registration can now be completed online through the e-Services Portal (Elektronikus Ügyintézés Portál), which has significantly simplified the process.

You will need to provide articles of association, details of company founders and members, information on the registered office, and proof of capital contribution. If the registered office is residential property, you may need consent from the property owner. A notarised document confirming capital contribution is typically required.

English-speaking lawyers in Hungary can handle the entire registration process on your behalf, ensuring all documentation is correct and submitted promptly. This avoids delays and rejections caused by incomplete or incorrectly formatted documents.

Capital Contribution and Bank Account Setup

When establishing a limited liability company, you must contribute the stated capital. For a standard Kft., this is typically 3,000 EUR or higher, depending on what you specify in your articles of association. Before company registration is finalised, you must transfer this capital to a company bank account. Hungarian banks require company registration documents to open accounts, so this becomes a sequential process.

Major Hungarian banks offering accounts to newly registered companies include OTP Bank, Erste Bank, and K&H Bank. Many have English-speaking staff in larger branches, particularly in Budapest. Some international banks also operate in Hungary and may offer account services to businesses. Compare fees and services before selecting a bank.

Opening a business account requires a company registration certificate, articles of association, identification documents for beneficial owners, and proof of address. Some banks may require additional documentation for foreign-owned businesses. Your lawyer or accountant can advise on specific bank requirements.

Tax Registration and Obligations

Once your company is registered, you must register for tax purposes. This involves obtaining a tax number from the National Tax and Customs Administration (Nemzeti Adó- és Vámhivatal, NAV). Registration is automatic for limited liability companies, but you should confirm registration and understand your specific tax obligations.

Hungary's corporate income tax rate is 19%, one of the lowest in Europe. However, various deductions and allowances may apply depending on your business activities. Small businesses and startups may qualify for preferential tax treatment in certain circumstances.

Value Added Tax (VAT) registration is required if your turnover exceeds certain thresholds (typically around 18,000 EUR annually). Once registered for VAT, you must file VAT returns quarterly or monthly and maintain detailed records of all transactions. The standard VAT rate is 27%, though reduced rates apply to certain goods and services.

As a business owner, you must file annual corporate tax returns and financial statements. These must be prepared in accordance with Hungarian accounting standards and, for companies above certain size thresholds, must be audited by a qualified auditor. An English-speaking accountant with experience in Hungarian business is invaluable for ensuring compliance and optimising your tax position.

Accounting and Financial Reporting Requirements

Hungarian law requires all businesses to maintain detailed accounting records. Limited liability companies must prepare annual financial statements including a balance sheet, profit and loss statement, and cash flow statement. These must be filed with the Court of Registration within specified timeframes after the financial year ends, typically within five months.

For companies with turnover above 30 million HUF (approximately 100,000 EUR), external audits are mandatory. Even for smaller companies, directors may be required to have accounts audited in certain circumstances. Understanding these requirements early helps you plan for accounting costs and ensure timely compliance.

Many foreign entrepreneurs use accounting firms specialising in small business bookkeeping. These firms can handle day-to-day accounting, preparation of financial statements, and tax return filing, allowing you to focus on running your business. English-speaking accountants are available throughout Hungary, particularly in Budapest.

Employment Law Considerations

If you plan to hire employees in Hungary, you must understand your obligations under Hungarian employment law. Employees must have written contracts specifying job duties, salary, working hours, and other material terms. Hungarian law provides extensive employee protections, including minimum wage guarantees, paid leave, statutory sick pay, and protection from unfair dismissal.

The standard working week in Hungary is 40 hours, though this can be modified within legal limits. Employees are entitled to at least 20 days paid annual leave, plus public holidays. Maternity and parental leave are provided, and employees cannot be dismissed for taking family leave.

As an employer, you must register employees with social security and health insurance authorities. You must withhold income tax and social contributions from employee salaries and remit these to the appropriate authorities. Failure to do so can result in substantial penalties and personal liability.

For foreign entrepreneurs unfamiliar with Hungarian employment law, consulting an English-speaking lawyer before hiring employees is essential. A lawyer can review employment contracts, advise on compliance, and assist with resolving disputes if they arise.

Intellectual Property and Corporate Protection

Protecting your company's intellectual property is important from inception. This may include registering trademarks for your business name and brand, obtaining copyright protection for original works, and protecting trade secrets through confidentiality agreements. Hungary is a member of the World Intellectual Property Organization and has procedures for trademark and patent registration.

For company names, you should conduct searches to ensure your chosen name is not already registered by another company. The Court of Registration provides name reservation services, allowing you to reserve a name while you complete registration formalities.

If your business involves proprietary technology, processes, or other valuable intellectual property, consider protection strategies with your lawyer. This may include trade secret protection, confidentiality agreements with employees and partners, and formal intellectual property registration where appropriate.

Corporate Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

After registration, your company remains subject to ongoing obligations. Annual financial statements must be filed, tax returns must be submitted, and you must maintain statutory books and records. Directors' personal liability for unpaid taxes or social contributions is a significant risk, so ensuring timely compliance is critical.

If your company structure includes multiple members or if you plan to raise external capital, you should establish internal governance procedures. This includes conducting annual shareholders' meetings, maintaining meeting minutes, and following formal procedures for significant decisions. Proper governance protects liability protection and demonstrates to banks and investors that your company is professionally managed.

Regular consultation with an English-speaking accountant and lawyer helps ensure ongoing compliance and allows you to stay informed of changes to Hungarian business law that may affect your operations.

Insurance and Risk Management

Various insurance policies protect your business and personal interests. Professional liability insurance (felelősségbiztosítás) is essential for service businesses. General commercial liability insurance protects against third-party injury or property damage claims. Directors and officers liability insurance protects individual directors from personal liability in certain circumstances.

For businesses involving substantial assets or liabilities, comprehensive insurance planning is prudent. Many English-speaking insurance brokers operate in Hungary and can assess your risks and recommend appropriate coverage.

Challenges and Common Pitfalls

Foreign entrepreneurs often encounter several challenges when establishing businesses in Hungary. Language barriers can slow processes if you don't speak Hungarian. Documentation requirements may exceed expectations, particularly for foreign-owned businesses or those involving international transactions. Understanding local business customs and regulatory expectations is essential for smooth operations.

A common pitfall is underestimating the cost of professional services. Hiring English-speaking lawyers and accountants involves expense, but the cost of non-compliance or poor business structure design far exceeds professional fees. Another pitfall is insufficient capitalisation; many startups fail because initial capital is insufficient to cover operating costs during the ramp-up phase.

Misunderstanding employee protections and wrongfully dismissing employees can result in substantial liability. Failing to register employees properly or maintain proper accounting records can expose you to penalties and personal liability. These risks are easily managed with proper legal and accounting advice.

Growth and Expansion

As your business grows, you may consider opening additional locations, hiring more employees, or expanding to other EU countries. Your lawyer can advise on optimising your corporate structure for growth, tax-efficient expansion, and compliance with regulations in new jurisdictions. Many successful foreign-owned businesses in Hungary leverage the EU framework to expand across Europe.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How long does it take to register a company in Hungary?
A: The registration process typically takes 7 to 10 working days when submitted online, though this can be faster if all documents are correctly prepared. Using a lawyer to prepare and submit documentation usually ensures the fastest possible registration.

Q: What is the minimum capital required for a limited liability company?
A: The minimum capital for a Kft. is typically around 3,000 EUR, though you can establish a company with higher capital if desired. The amount should reflect your intended business operations and investor confidence.

Q: Do I need to be physically present in Hungary to register a company?
A: No. Most of the registration process can be handled remotely through lawyers or accountants. However, some bank requirements may require your physical presence for account opening and verification.

Q: What are the tax implications of setting up a business in Hungary as a foreigner?
A: Taxation depends on where you are a tax resident. If you are a Hungarian tax resident, your company's profits are subject to 19% corporate income tax. If you are not a Hungarian tax resident, different rules may apply. Consult an accountant about your specific situation.

Q: Can foreign nationals own 100% of a Hungarian company?
A: Yes. Foreign nationals can be the sole founder and member of a limited liability company. There are no restrictions on foreign ownership of most business types, though some sectors (defence, media in certain cases) may have restrictions.

Q: What happens if I cannot meet payroll or tax obligations?
A: Failure to meet these obligations can result in penalties, interest, and personal liability for directors. If your company faces financial difficulty, consult a lawyer immediately about restructuring, insolvency procedures, or other options available under Hungarian law.
