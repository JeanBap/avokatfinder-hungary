Essential Documents for Moving to Hungary: The Complete Expat Guide (2026)

Introduction: Navigating Hungary's Document Maze

Moving to Hungary is an exciting adventure. Budapest's Danube charm, the thermal spas of Eger, vibrant expat communities in Pest, and increasingly remote-friendly visa policies make Hungary an attractive destination for digital nomads, professionals, and families alike. Yet beneath the romantic surface lies a bureaucratic landscape that catches many expats off guard.

Unlike some Western European countries with streamlined relocation processes, Hungary requires a specific sequence of documents and registrations before you can legally establish residency, access healthcare, pay taxes, open a bank account, or rent a property. Missing even one document can cascade into delays: your tax ID depends on your lakcímkártya, your healthcare registration depends on your TAJ card, your banking depends on your address card. Each authority moves at its own pace, and navigating between them without English-speaking support can take weeks.

This guide covers every document you'll need, in the order you actually need them. We've interviewed expat forums, tax professionals, and lawyers who handle Hungarian relocations daily. We've also included the AvökatFinder Concierge option—because many expats simply don't have the patience or language skills to chase multiple government offices for weeks.

The document timeline typically unfolds like this: before departure, you secure apostille-certified copies of your birth certificate, marriage/divorce records, and diplomas. Upon arrival, you register your address (lakcímkártya), obtain your social security number (TAJ card), apply for your tax ID (adóazonosító), and register with health insurance (OEP). Within your first two weeks, you can open a bank account and sign a tenancy agreement if your accommodation is lined up. Within a month, you have the legal foundation to work, rent, and access Hungarian healthcare.

For most expats, this timeline can compress to 1-2 weeks with professional help. Without it, expect 4-8 weeks of back-and-forth, missed appointments, and documents returned for missing stamps or signatures.

Before You Leave Home: Critical Pre-Departure Documents

The biggest mistake expats make is arriving in Hungary without apostille-certified documents. You cannot get these in Hungary. You must obtain them from your home country authorities before you leave. If you forget, you'll either delay your registration by months or pay significant fees to have documents couriered to you—or worse, lose out on job opportunities or apartment rentals while you wait.

Apostille Certification: The Non-Negotiable First Step

Hungary is a signatory to the Hague Convention on Apostilles. This means your home country can issue a simple certification (called an apostille) that authenticates your documents for use in Hungary without the need for additional consular certification. This process is fast, cheap, and essential.

An apostille is a one-page certification attached to your original document by your home country's authorized authority—typically the Secretary of State, state records office, or local court clerk. It certifies only that the document is genuine. It doesn't translate it, and it doesn't grant you rights; it just authenticates the signature.

Documents that absolutely need apostille certification:

  • Birth certificate (full, long-form version—do not use short form)
  • Marriage certificate (if married)
  • Divorce decree or decree absolute (if divorced)
  • University degree and diplomas (original or official copy)
  • Police clearance / criminal record check (from your home country)
  • Professional licenses (nursing, engineering, law, etc., if relevant)
  • Powers of attorney (if you authorize someone to act on your behalf)

Order apostille certificates from your home country 6-8 weeks before you move. In the US, contact your Secretary of State or local county clerk. In the UK, order from the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office. In Canada, contact your provincial government office. Processing times vary from 1-3 weeks, plus courier time.

Pro tip: Order 2-3 extra copies of each document. Bureaucrats lose papers. You'll thank yourself later.

Professional Translations

Hungarian authorities accept documents in English if you include a certified Hungarian translation. The translation must be done by a translator registered with the Hungarian Chamber of Translators and Interpreters (MKIK). You cannot translate documents yourself, and notarized translations don't count.

If your documents are in English, you technically don't need a Hungarian translation for initial residency registration (immigration officials speak English). However, for tax registration and healthcare, Hungarian translations are required. Plan to have translations done locally in Budapest once you arrive—expect EUR 20-50 per page depending on complexity.

If your documents are in another language, you must arrange professional Hungarian translation before your registration appointments.

Valid Passport and Travel Documents

Your passport must be valid for the entire duration of your stay. EU/EEA citizens can stay indefinitely; non-EU nationals typically receive a visa for 90 days (tourist), up to one year (work), or multiple years (residence permit). Ensure your visa is correct before boarding your flight.

For non-EU nationals: You may need a long-stay visa from the Hungarian embassy in your home country. This is different from a tourist visa and is essential if you plan to stay longer than 90 days or work in Hungary. Apply 6-8 weeks before departure.

Health Insurance and Medical Records

Bring copies of your current health insurance documents, vaccination records, and any prescriptions or medical summaries from your home country doctor. Hungary's public health system requires registration before you can access GP services, but private clinics can see you immediately.

Have prescriptions translated into Hungarian or bring the generic drug names. Hungarian pharmacies operate differently than UK/US pharmacies—you often need a prescription for medications that are over-the-counter elsewhere.

Financial and Employment Documents

If you're relocating for a job, bring:

  • Original employment contract (and a certified Hungarian translation if not in English)
  • Proof of income (last 3 months' payslips or salary confirmation letter)
  • References from previous employers (helpful for opening bank accounts and rental agreements)

If self-employed or freelance, bring:

  • Bank statements showing regular income (last 6 months)
  • Portfolio or client testimonials
  • Details of your previous business registration or tax filings

If you have cryptocurrency or digital assets, document your ownership proofs—Hungary's tax authority (NAV) increasingly scrutinizes digital income sources.

Residency Registration: Hungary's Address Card (Lakcímkártya)

The lakcímkártya (address card) is Hungary's residency document. It's issued by your local government office (polgármesteri hivatal or közszolgáltatási iroda) in your district. Until you have this card, you're technically not officially registered as living in Hungary, which blocks everything else.

What You Need for Lakcímkártya Registration

Gather these documents and schedule an appointment at your local government office:

The lakcímkártya is issued immediately (same day) if all documents are in order. You receive a temporary certificate, and the plastic card arrives by mail in 5-10 business days. Use the temporary certificate for all subsequent registrations until the card arrives.

Registering Without a Fixed Address

If you haven't secured an apartment yet, you can register at a temporary address (hotel, hostel, Airbnb) while you house-hunt. Some expats use their employer's office address or a friend's address temporarily. The government office staff will advise on whether your temporary accommodation qualifies. Once you sign a tenancy agreement, you update your address on the lakcímkártya.

TAJ Card: Hungary's Social Security Number

The TAJ card (Társadalombiztosítási Azonosító Jegy) is your social security number—a 9-digit identifier used for all healthcare and pension purposes. You cannot access Hungarian healthcare without it.

You apply for the TAJ card at the Government Insurance office (Társadalombiztosítási Ügyfélszolgálat) or via your employer if you're working. Non-working residents (students, remote workers for foreign companies, retirees) can apply independently.

Required documents for TAJ card application:

  • Lakcímkártya (or temporary certificate)
  • Valid passport
  • Apostilled birth certificate (certified Hungarian translation if not in Hungarian)
  • Completed TAJ application form (available at the office)

Processing time: 5-10 business days. You'll receive a notice letter with your TAJ number immediately; the physical card arrives by mail.

Adóazonosító: Tax ID Registration

Your adóazonosító (tax identification number) is issued by the National Tax Authority (Nemzeti Adó- és Vámhivatal, NAV). This 10-digit number is required to open a bank account, sign a rental agreement, and work in Hungary. You cannot legally earn income without it.

Documents needed for tax ID application:

  • Lakcímkártya (address card)
  • Valid passport
  • Completed tax ID application form (NAV 1-es nyomtatvány, available online or at NAV offices)
  • Proof of address (lakcímkártya sufficient)

EU/EEA citizens typically get the tax ID same-day at NAV offices in Budapest (District I or VII). Non-EU citizens may face additional screening and could wait 1-2 weeks.

You can apply online on the NAV website if you have a digital signature or eBanking credentials, but first-time applicants usually need to visit in person.

OEP Registration: Health Insurance Enrollment

The OEP (Országos Egészségbiztosítási Pénztár) is Hungary's state health insurance fund. Once you have your TAJ card and lakcímkártya, you're automatically enrolled in the OEP. However, certain categories of residents (non-working EU citizens, digital nomads, self-employed) may need to apply separately.

Who must apply to OEP:

  • Self-employed persons (must register as a self-employed professional with NAV first)
  • Artists and freelancers
  • EU/EEA citizens not working in Hungary
  • Non-EU nationals on residence permits not tied to employment

Documents for OEP enrollment:

  • Completed OEP enrollment form (Biztosított nyilvántartásba vétel nyomtatvány)
  • TAJ card or temporary TAJ letter
  • Proof of income or self-employment registration
  • ID and address card

Once enrolled, you have access to Hungary's public healthcare system: GP visits, hospital care, and prescription medications at subsidized rates. Enrollment is typically free, though self-employed persons pay approximately 8% of their declared income as health contributions.

Tax and Fiscal ID Registration: Understanding Hungary's Tax System

Hungary's tax system is relatively straightforward compared to some EU countries, but expats often stumble on reporting requirements and deadlines.

Income Tax Registration and Filing

All residents earning income in Hungary must file an annual tax return by May 31st following the year of income. Employees typically have taxes withheld by their employer, but you still must file to claim deductions or refunds.

Hungary's personal income tax rates are:

  • 15% for employees (flat rate)
  • 19.5% for self-employed and freelancers (including social contributions)
  • 0% for the first HUF 3.6 million (approximately EUR 10,000) for certain low-income earners

Documents required for tax filing:

  • Adóazonosító (tax ID)
  • Evidence of income (payslips, invoices, bank statements)
  • Proof of expenses (if self-employed)
  • Completed tax return form (available on NAV website or from an accountant)

Most expats hire a local accountant (könyvelő) for approximately EUR 50-150 per month to handle monthly tax reporting and annual filing. This is highly recommended if you're self-employed or have complex income sources.

VAT Registration (if applicable)

If you're self-employed and your annual turnover exceeds HUF 18 million (approximately EUR 50,000), you must register for VAT (ÁFA – Általános Forgalmi Adó) and file quarterly VAT returns.

VAT rates in Hungary are:

  • 27% standard rate (highest in the EU)
  • 18% reduced rate (foodstuffs, medicines, books)
  • 5% super-reduced rate (certain essential goods)

If you don't reach the turnover threshold, VAT registration is optional but has advantages for business-to-business transactions.

Property Tax (If Owning Real Estate)

If you purchase property in Hungary, you'll pay an annual property tax (kommunális adó). The tax varies by municipality but is typically 0.1-0.3% of the property's assessed value, paid to the local government office.

You'll also pay capital gains tax (18% or 19.5% depending on circumstances) if you sell the property within 5 years.

Banking: Opening a Hungarian Bank Account

Opening a bank account in Hungary requires your lakcímkártya, tax ID (adóazonosító), and valid passport. Most expat-friendly banks in Budapest are OTP, MKB, Erste Bank, and K&H. They typically have English-speaking staff at major branches.

Documents needed:

  • Lakcímkártya (address card)
  • Valid passport
  • Adóazonosító (tax ID)
  • Proof of income (optional; required if you're applying for credit or loans)
  • Proof of address (lakcímkártya sufficient)

Account setup typically takes 30-60 minutes. Online banking (NetBank) is standard and available in English. Monthly fees range from EUR 0-3 depending on the bank and account type.

Expat-friendly banks in Budapest:

  • Wise (formerly TransferWise): No physical branches, but offers Hungarian IBAN and low international transfer fees. Account opening online.
  • OTP Bank: Largest bank in Hungary; most ATMs. Account opening at any branch.
  • Erste Bank: Good for expats; English-speaking staff. Multiple Budapest branches.
  • MKB Bank: Competitive rates; popular with expats. Central locations in Budapest.

Many expats use Wise or similar fintech banks for international transfers, and a Hungarian bank account for local bills and salary deposits.

Healthcare Registration: Accessing Hungary's Medical System

Once you have your TAJ card, you're enrolled in Hungary's public healthcare system. Choose a general practitioner (háziorvos) by registering with a GP's office. GPs are gatekeepers to specialist care and hospitals—you cannot access a specialist without a GP referral.

Find a GP in your district by visiting your local health center or searching the NAV/OEP website. Most GPs have waiting lists of 1-2 weeks for new patients, but can see you within 24 hours for urgent matters.

Documents to bring to your GP registration appointment:

  • TAJ card (or temporary letter)
  • Lakcímkártya (address card)
  • Passport
  • Medical history and vaccination records (in Hungarian translation if not in English)
  • Current medications list (generic names in Hungarian)

Public healthcare in Hungary is comprehensive and inexpensive—GP visits are free, hospital care is free, and prescriptions are heavily subsidized (usually EUR 2-5 per medication). However, waiting lists for specialists and surgeries can extend 2-4 weeks.

Many expats supplement public healthcare with private insurance (Allianz, Axa, Groupama) for faster specialist access. Private insurance costs EUR 30-80 per month depending on coverage.

Property and Rental Documentation: Signing a Tenancy Agreement

Hungary's rental market is informal by Western standards. Many rentals are unregistered with the tax authorities, which creates risk for both tenant and landlord. If you're renting, ensure your agreement is written and includes key terms.

Essential elements of a Hungarian tenancy agreement:

  • Names and IDs of both parties (landlord and tenant)
  • Full address of the property
  • Rental period (start and end dates)
  • Monthly rent and payment schedule
  • Deposit amount (typically 1-2 months' rent)
  • Utility costs (heating, water, electricity) and who pays
  • Maintenance and repair responsibilities
  • Termination notice period (typically 30-60 days)
  • Signatures of both parties and a witness (if notarized)

Get the agreement in writing even if the landlord is informal. Have a Hungarian lawyer review it (EUR 100-200 for review). Ensure the landlord provides a receipt for your deposit and proof of payment.

Documents landlords typically request from you:

  • Proof of income (payslip or bank statements)
  • Employment contract
  • Passport or ID
  • References from previous landlords or employers
  • Proof of solvency (bank balance, credit reference)

Once you've signed a tenancy agreement, you can update your address on your lakcímkártya. Give your landlord a copy of the agreement for their records.

Tax implications for landlords and tenants: If the landlord is registered as a property rental business, they may be required to register you with the tax authority and issue you an invoice for rent. If they're not registered, they're operating illegally and you have limited recourse if disputes arise. Always verify the landlord's legitimacy before signing.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the entire registration process take in Hungary?

Without professional help: 4-8 weeks. With AvökatFinder concierge: 2-3 weeks. The timeline depends on how quickly government offices process your applications and how fast you can gather apostille documents. Having everything pre-prepared before arrival significantly accelerates the process.

Can I work in Hungary on a tourist visa?

Technically no, but enforcement is weak for remote workers employed by foreign companies. You cannot legally accept work from a Hungarian employer or work in Hungary for a Hungarian entity on a tourist visa. However, many digital nomads work remotely for their home country employer on a tourist visa without issues. For legality and tax compliance, apply for a long-stay visa or residence permit before arrival.

Is it better to rent or buy property in Hungary?

For most first-time expats, renting is recommended. Buying requires additional documentation (foreign national investment approval, property deed registration, title insurance), additional taxes (transfer tax, stamp duty), and a long-term commitment. Rent for your first 6-12 months, then decide about purchasing. Budapest's property market has appreciated 10-15% annually for expats, but stability matters more than timing for most.

Do I need travel health insurance before arriving in Hungary?

Yes. Your home country health insurance may not cover you in Hungary. Purchase travel health insurance before departure that covers at least the first month until you're registered with the OEP. Most policies cost EUR 20-40 per month and cover emergency care, GP visits, and hospitalization.

What happens if I arrive without apostille documents?

Your lakcímkártya application will be rejected or delayed until you obtain them. You can apply through your home country embassy in Budapest (expensive and slow, 4-6 weeks) or have someone mail the originals to you (1-2 weeks for courier). Plan ahead and avoid this situation entirely.
